Research Question

How do high-net-worth donors evaluate political giving opportunities under conditions of uncertainty and constraint, and what decision-making frameworks shape how they allocate resources across political organizations?

For the purposes of this study, "political organizations" includes candidate committees, party committees, PACs and Super PACs, 501(c)(4) advocacy organizations, and 527s. The research focuses on four dimensions of donor decision-making: evaluation criteria, information sources, allocation logic, and the role of personal and professional relationships.

The project is conducted in partnership with Majority Rising NC and is descriptive in orientation. It seeks to surface how donors actually navigate a high-uncertainty decision environment, rather than to prescribe what they should do.

Current Sources

The project draws on three categories of sources.

Interview subjects. The primary source of original data is a set of semi-structured interviews with high-net-worth political donors and individuals with direct visibility into donor decision-making, including advisors, intermediaries, and senior staff at political organizations. Interviews are conducted on a confidential basis under the terms described below.

Academic and practitioner literature. The project is grounded in a literature review spanning political finance, elite philanthropy, information asymmetry and principal-agent theory, donor collaboratives, and strategic philanthropy. Primary analytical frameworks are information asymmetry theory and, secondarily, portfolio theory.

Public data and organizational materials. Where useful, the project draws on Federal Election Commission filings, IRS Form 990 filings, and publicly available reporting on donor networks and political giving.

A full bibliography will be included in the final capstone report.

Anonymity & Data Protection

Protecting the confidentiality of interview subjects is central to the integrity of this project. The following protocols apply:

Anonymity. No interview subject will be named in the final capstone report or in any associated materials without their explicit written consent. Where direct quotes or specific examples are used, identifying details will be removed or generalized so that subjects cannot be identified by readers familiar with the field.

Recording and transcription. With the subject's consent, conversations are transcribed using privacy-vetted tools selected to minimize third-party data exposure. Audio is not retained beyond the transcription process. Subjects who prefer not to be recorded can opt for notes-only interviews.

Data handling. Interview transcripts and notes are stored on encrypted, password-protected storage accessible only to the researcher. Identifying information is kept separately from interview content.

Use of materials. Interview content is used solely for the purposes of this capstone project. It will not be shared with Majority Rising NC, Duke faculty advisors, or any third party in a form that identifies individual subjects.

Subject control. Interview subjects may withdraw from the project at any point and may request that specific statements be removed from the record. Subjects will have the opportunity to review any direct quotes attributed to them, anonymized or otherwise, before publication.

Questions about these protocols can be directed to Ian Shannon at ian.shannon@duke.edu or to the project's faculty advisors at the Duke Sanford School of Public Policy.
